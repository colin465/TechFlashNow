Why the NSA Is Urging Millions to Restart Their Routers Right Now

By TechFlashNow Staff Published 6 min read

The National Security Agency is urging Americans to restart and secure their home internet routers after U.S. officials said Russian military intelligence hackers used vulnerable devices in a broad operation to intercept sensitive information.

The latest warning came after the Justice Department and FBI announced a court-authorized disruption of a network of compromised small-office and home-office routers that officials tied to Russia’s GRU, specifically Unit 26165, also known as APT28 or Fancy Bear.

What triggered the alert was not a routine consumer-security reminder but a government response to an active espionage campaign. According to U.S. and allied agencies, the hackers had been exploiting vulnerable routers since at least 2024, including TP-Link devices affected by known flaws, then changing settings in ways that let them observe or redirect traffic.

Why officials are sounding the alarm now

Federal investigators said the campaign relied on small routers and other internet-connected devices that are often overlooked by users once they are installed. Many of these devices remain online for years without firmware updates, password changes or basic security review.

Officials warned that a simple reboot can disrupt certain temporary malware and sever malicious connections, but they also stressed that restarting alone is not enough. Users are being told to install security patches, replace default credentials, disable remote administration if it is not needed and turn on encryption features where available.

Security agencies have also encouraged users to check whether their device is still supported by the manufacturer. Older consumer routers often stop receiving firmware updates long before consumers realize support has ended.

How the attack worked

Authorities said the compromised devices were part of a botnet used to conceal the origin of malicious traffic and support broader intelligence-gathering efforts. By routing operations through hijacked routers, attackers could mask their true location while scanning targets or attempting to breach organizations in the United States and Europe.

In some cases, the attackers reportedly exploited known vulnerabilities that had patches available but were never installed by end users. Once inside, they could change DNS settings, route commands through the device or deploy malware that survived until the system was reset or reconfigured.

That kind of access can create a quiet but serious risk for households and small businesses. A compromised router can potentially expose login sessions, redirect users to fraudulent responses for certain sites and harvest data that users would normally expect to be protected.

How this could affect users

Users may not notice any obvious symptoms if their router has been targeted. Internet service can still appear normal even when a device is being used to relay traffic or maintain persistence for remote access.

That is part of what makes router compromises difficult to detect. Warning signs may include unexpected settings changes, unfamiliar administrator logins, unexplained DNS values, performance instability or management features becoming enabled without the user’s knowledge.

Experts say consumers should review router admin panels, enable automatic updates where possible, verify DNS settings and replace devices that are at end-of-support or no longer receive security updates.

What to watch next

While the recent disruption removed part of the operation, officials emphasized that similar campaigns are likely to continue as long as insecure edge devices remain online and unmaintained.

The warning is also a reminder that cyber risk increasingly begins at home. Routers, webcams and other always-on devices can become attractive footholds because they sit at the edge of personal and business networks yet are rarely checked after setup.

The practical takeaway from officials is straightforward: restart the device, install updates, change passwords and confirm your router is still supported. For many households, that may be the fastest way to reduce risk from a threat most users never see coming.
